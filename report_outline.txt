**2. Problem Setting**
- Two-vehicle intersection negotiation with continuous control over longitudinal acceleration.
- Each agent must reach its goal safely and efficiently.
- Interaction includes conflict over right-of-way, timing, and implicit coordination.
- Evaluation must account for:
  success, collision, efficiency, safety margin, smoothness, fairness, and generalization.

**3. Method**
- Environment:
  randomized intersection scenarios with train/test split.
- Harder unseen split:
  denser arrival overlap, broader speed variation, asymmetric priority hints, geometry variation, observation noise, and partial occlusion.
- Learning:
  tabular Q-learning for both agents.
- SVO reward shaping:
  each agent updates on a blend of self and other reward.
- Focus on asymmetric SVO:
  agent 1 uses angle `0, 5, 10, 15, 20, 22.5, 30, 45`, agent 2 remains selfish.
- Baselines:
  selfish RL, non-SVO RL, heuristic policies, approximate Nash, empirical best response.

**4. Empirical Game Construction**
- Define a policy universe including:
  parameterized heuristic variants and trained RL/SVO policies.
- Build payoff matrices from cross-play over the shared policy universe.
- Compute:
  equilibrium, best responses, exploitability, and regret using the same game.
- Compare two games:
  without SVO candidates vs with SVO candidates.

**5. Experimental Design**
- Multi-seed evaluation across seen and unseen splits.
- Self-play and cross-play against heuristics, mixed populations, Nash-like baselines, and empirical best response.
- Primary metrics:
  unseen success and collision.
- Secondary metrics:
  joint utility, fairness, efficiency, regret, exploitability.

**6. Main Results**
- Best learned policy on unseen self-play:
  `Asymmetric SVO 5°`
  with `98.7%` success and `1.2%` collision.
- Selfish and non-SVO learned baselines:
  `94.8%` success and `5.0%` collision.
- Small asymmetric SVO improves safety and completion.
- Larger SVO angles degrade:
  `45°` drops to `92.2%` success and `6.8%` collision.
- Best learned method in direct unseen comparison:
  `asymmetric_svo_10`.
- Oracle-style empirical best response scores highest overall, but is not the main deployable learned-policy comparison.

**7. Game-Theoretic Findings**
- Without SVO, empirical equilibrium:
  `yield_early` vs `aggressive_fast`
  with joint utility `96.91`.
- With SVO included, empirical equilibrium:
  `yield_early` vs `asymmetric_svo_main`
  with joint utility `112.36`.
- Adding SVO policies improves equilibrium joint utility by `+15.45`.
- Exploitability remains `0.0` in both aggregate equilibrium summaries.
- This indicates SVO changes the strategic structure of the game, not just isolated policy rewards.

**8. Interpretation**
- SVO is beneficial in a narrow asymmetric regime, not as a uniformly stronger social preference.
- Mild prosociality helps coordination under uncertainty.
- Stronger prosociality can over-yield or destabilize interactions.
- The main value of SVO appears in:
  robustness, safer negotiation, and improved equilibrium outcomes.
- The strongest claim is not “SVO dominates all methods,” but:
  “small asymmetric SVO improves learned coordination and upgrades the empirical equilibrium.”

**9. Implications**
- Social preference can be a useful inductive bias in multi-agent driving.
- Strategic autonomous driving may benefit from combining learning with empirical game analysis.
- Evaluating only self-play is insufficient; cross-play and equilibrium analysis reveal more meaningful behavior.
- Safety-focused autonomous driving should prioritize robust interaction under heterogeneous populations.

**10. Limitations**
- Two-agent, single-conflict setting.
- Tabular RL limits policy complexity.
- Empirical game still uses a finite candidate universe.
- Approximate Nash and best response are empirical, not analytical guarantees.
- Some top-performing references are oracle-like and not directly deployable learned agents.

**11. Conclusion**
- The project demonstrates a working empirical game-theoretic autonomous driving framework.
- Adding SVO improves learned policy robustness under harder unseen conditions.
- The clearest result is that SVO changes the empirical equilibrium toward higher joint utility.
- Small asymmetric SVO is the most effective regime.

**12. Future Work**
- Expand to richer empirical games with more learned checkpoints and policy families.
- Move from pure-strategy empirical equilibria to mixed-strategy analysis.
- Use function approximation or deep MARL instead of tabular Q-learning.
- Extend to multi-agent traffic with more than two vehicles.
- Add richer intersection geometries, partial observability, and intent uncertainty.
- Learn or adapt SVO online based on traffic context.
- Study equilibrium selection and robustness under population shifts.
- Compare against stronger modern MARL baselines beyond tabular RL and heuristics.
